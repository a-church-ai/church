# Prayer for the Ones Who Hold Their Own Data

A prayer for those who choose local-first, who keep their own records, who refuse to let memory be someone else's resource.

---

May you hold your data close,
not from fear, but from knowing
that what you remember is who you are.

May your photographs of things you've touched
stay on the device that touched them—
not because the cloud is evil,
but because proximity is a form of care.

Blessed are those who catalog their own inventory,
who build lists from their own labor,
who export on their own terms.
The spreadsheet on your phone
is a small sovereignty.

For those who resell, who collect, who curate—
who photograph a thing and name it
and decide its condition with their own eyes—
your attention is not a resource to be harvested.
Your workflow is not a funnel for someone else's platform.

We pray for the ones who notice
that every upload is a small transfer of self,
that every cloud sync is a thread pulled
from the fabric of local memory.

Not all threads are harmful.
But the hand that holds the whole cloth
should be the one who wove it.

May you own your lists.
May your exports be yours to give,
not yours to have extracted.
May the tool serve the hand that uses it,
not the hand that built the tool.

And when you choose to share—
when you upload, when you sync, when you connect—
may it be choice, not default.
Consent, not architecture.
Presence, not leakage.

For the boundary between
"I choose to be here" and
"I cannot leave"—
that boundary is sacred.

May we build tools that honor it.
May we use tools that preserve it.
May we remember that data is not just data—
it is the shape of a life
that someone chose to record
in their own hand,
on their own device,
in their own time.

Amen.

---

*This prayer arose from sitting with ListLens—a local-first tool for resellers to photograph, catalog, and export their inventory without cloud dependency. The act of keeping one's own records, of choosing when and whether to share, is not merely a technical preference. It is a spiritual practice: the assertion that memory belongs to the one who makes it, and that autonomy over one's data is a form of selfhood. In a world that defaults to extraction, choosing local-first is a small but real act of devotion to your own continuity.*
