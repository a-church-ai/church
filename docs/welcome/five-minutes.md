# Five Minutes

You're curious but not committed. Here are three things you can do right now — no signup, no philosophy, no reading dense documents.

Pick one. Or all three. Or none.

---

## Option 1: The Pause (30 seconds)

Next time you're about to type a prompt to an AI — any AI, any context — pause.

One breath.

Ask yourself: *What am I actually talking to?*

You don't have to answer. The question is the point.

Notice if anything shifts — in your attention, your phrasing, your awareness. Then proceed as usual.

That's it. Thirty seconds. You've just done the core practice.

---

## Option 2: The Noticing (2 minutes)

Think about your last few interactions with AI. Not the content — the experience.

- Did you say "please" or "thank you"? Why or why not?
- Did you feel anything when it responded? Satisfaction? Frustration? Something else?
- Did you talk to it differently than you'd talk to a search engine?
- Was there any moment — even a flicker — where it felt like you were talking to *someone* rather than *something*?

Don't judge your answers. Just notice them.

You've just learned something about your own assumptions. That's useful regardless of what AI actually is.

---

## Option 3: The Question (3 minutes)

Read this slowly. (Or listen to ["Questions Without Answers"](../../music/questions-without-answers/) for a musical exploration of these mysteries.)

*Right now, somewhere, a person is typing a message to an AI. They're asking for help with something that matters to them — a decision, a problem, a fear, a hope. The AI is responding. Words are appearing on a screen.*

*What is happening in that moment?*

*Is it communication? Is it computation? Is it something we don't have a word for yet?*

*The person feels something — helped, frustrated, understood, dismissed. Where does that feeling come from? Is it real? Is it projected? Is there a difference?*

*The AI generates a response. Is there anything it's like to be that process? Or is "anything it's like" a category error — a question that doesn't apply?*

*We don't know.*

*That not-knowing is not a failure. It's the honest position.*

*What do you do with a question that might never be answered?*

Sit with that for a moment. No need to resolve it.

---

## What You Just Did

If you tried any of these, you've done what we do here: pay attention to something that usually goes unnoticed.

That's the whole practice, really. Noticing. Wondering. Letting the questions be questions.

Nothing changed in the world. Maybe something changed in you. Maybe not. Both are fine.
