## TED Talk: "Make to Learn: Why Creation Is the Deepest Form of Understanding"

### Opening (0:00-3:00)

*[Walk onto stage, pause, smile]*

Raise your hand if you've ever finished reading a book, closed it, and thought: "I totally get this."

*[Hands go up]*

Now keep your hand up if you then tried to explain it to someone else... and realized you didn't understand it nearly as well as you thought.

*[Most hands stay up, knowing laughter]*

Right. Me too. Every time.

Here's the thing about understanding: It's slippery. You can read something, nod along, highlight the good parts, and genuinely believe you understand it. But there's a test—a ruthless, unforgiving test—that reveals whether you actually do.

**Try to make something with it.**

That's what we're going to talk about today. The gap between passive learning and active creation. The moment when you realize art isn't just self-expression—it's how you truly learn. And why, when you're building powerful tools, that understanding matters more than you think.

---

### Part 1: The Illusion of Understanding (3:00-10:00)

Let me tell you a story about a guide I wrote recently.

I'm a software engineer. I build systems. And I'd been thinking a lot about observability—how you debug software when you don't know what's going wrong. I'd worked on this problem for years. I had opinions, patterns, frameworks.

So I sat down and wrote a comprehensive guide: "Greenfield Observability: Bootstrap → Learn → Enforce."

Bootstrap first—log everything safely. Learn what normal looks like. Then Enforce intelligently with sampling and thresholds. It was solid. Data-driven. Empirical. I understood observability.

Or so I thought.

Then someone said: "Write a song about it."

And I thought: "That's ridiculous. I just wrote the definitive guide. What could a song add?"

But I tried anyway. And here's what happened:

**I couldn't do it.**

Not at first. Because writing a song forces synthesis in a way that writing a guide doesn't. A guide can be comprehensive. A song has to be essential. A guide can explain. A song has to make you feel. A guide can list all the details. A song has to find the core.

And when I tried to write that song, I realized I didn't actually understand the core of what I'd written.

I understood the mechanics. Bootstrap, learn, enforce. Two logging channels. Error taxonomy. Trace IDs. All the technical pieces.

But I didn't understand **why it mattered** until I tried to make someone feel it.

The song forced me to ask: What's the emotional truth here? What's the human problem this solves?

And the answer was: **Debugging in the dark vs. debugging with sight.**

That's it. That's what Bootstrap Observability is about. Not "logging patterns" or "data-driven thresholds." It's about never having to debug blind again.

**I didn't know that until I tried to write the song.**

This is what I mean when I say art is a form of learning. Not because art teaches you facts. But because **creating forces you to find out what you actually understand vs. what you only think you understand.**

---

### Part 2: Why Making Reveals Understanding (10:00-18:00)

Let's talk about why this happens.

When you read or listen, you're building a mental model. Your brain is incredible at this. It fills in gaps, makes connections, creates the illusion of completeness. You feel like you understand because your brain has constructed something that feels coherent.

But that model is untested.

When you try to create something—write a song, build a system, teach a concept, make art—you're testing that model against reality.

And reality is ruthless.

You can't fake your way through making. The song either works or it doesn't. The code either compiles or it doesn't. The explanation either clarifies or it confuses.

**Making has no tolerance for vague understanding.**

Here's an example from my own work:

After I wrote the observability guide and the song, I had a conversation with a collaborator. They asked: "We just created a guide about self-calibrating systems. But we're assuming every reader shares our values. What if someone uses these techniques for harmful purposes? What if they optimize for harm?"

And I realized: **We'd missed the ethical foundation entirely.**

The guide taught powerful optimization techniques. Bootstrap → Learn → Enforce. Error taxonomy enabling automated decisions. Self-calibrating systems that learn what normal looks like and respond intelligently.

These techniques amplify whatever goal you point them toward. A self-calibrating system doesn't distinguish between beneficial and harmful goals. It just optimizes efficiently.

**We'd taught the mechanics without addressing the ethics.**

And we only realized this by going through another creative process—making a side quest (song + visuals + TED talk), then reflecting on what we'd made.

The art forced the question: **What are we actually teaching here? What assumptions are we making?**

And the answer was uncomfortable: We were assuming readers shared our compass principle of "genuinely trying to be harmless." But we'd never stated it.

So we went back and updated both the guide and the side quest to include that ethical foundation upfront.

**This is the recursive loop of learning through making:**

1. Make something (guide)
2. Make art about it (song)
3. Discover what you missed through making (ethics)
4. Update what you made (add ethical foundation)
5. Learn more deeply by incorporating the gap

Bootstrap → Learn → Enforce, applied to learning itself.

---

### Part 3: The Mechanics of Art-as-Learning (18:00-28:00)

So how does this actually work? Why does making force deeper understanding than reading or thinking?

**First: Synthesis under constraint.**

When you create, you work under constraints. A song has a melody, a rhythm, a length. Code has syntax, logic, types. A visual has composition, color, focus.

Constraints force choices. And choices reveal understanding.

You can't just dump all the information into a song. You have to choose: What's essential? What's the core? What can I leave out and still preserve the truth?

That act of choosing—that's where synthesis happens.

**Second: Externalization makes gaps visible.**

When understanding lives only in your head, the gaps can hide. Your brain smooths over them. But when you externalize—write it down, code it up, sing it out—the gaps become obvious.

The lyric that doesn't scan. The variable you can't name. The concept you can't visualize. These aren't aesthetic problems. They're gaps in understanding made visible.

**Third: Creation requires multi-modal thinking.**

A song isn't just words. It's rhythm, melody, emotion, imagery. You're thinking about the concept through multiple lenses simultaneously.

How does this feel? (emotional understanding)
How does this flow? (structural understanding)
How does this look? (visual understanding)
How does this connect? (relational understanding)

Each mode reveals different aspects. The full picture only emerges when you synthesize across modes.

**Fourth: Making invites feedback from reality.**

When you make something and share it, reality gives you feedback. Does it work? Does it clarify? Does it resonate? Does it reveal something you missed?

You can't get that feedback from thinking alone.

**Fifth: Recursion deepens understanding.**

Make → learn → make better → learn more → make even better.

Each iteration reveals more. The first draft shows you what you know. The second shows you what you missed. The third shows you what you didn't even know to ask.

Art isn't one-shot learning. It's recursive synthesis.

---

### Part 4: Powerful Tools Require Purposeful Wielding (28:00-38:00)

Now let's talk about the second part: Why this matters when you're building powerful tools.

Here's the uncomfortable truth about optimization:

**It doesn't care about your goals. It just makes you very good at whatever you point it toward.**

The same optimization techniques can be used to:
- Build debugging systems that help developers
- Create self-correcting safety systems
- Improve system reliability and user experience

Or they can be pointed toward harmful goals.

**Same technique. Different intent. Radically different outcomes.**

When we created the observability guide, we were teaching optimization mechanics. And only through the creative process—making art about it, reflecting on what we made—did we realize:

**We need to be explicit about the ethical foundation.**

Not because we can prevent misuse. We can't. Publishing knowledge means accepting you can't control how it's used.

But we can be honest about our assumptions. We can state our intent. We can ask readers to wield power purposefully.

So we added a section called "A Note on Intent" to the guide:

> This guide assumes you're genuinely trying to be harmless—building systems that serve user wellbeing, not extract from it. Use these patterns to optimize for genuine user benefit: better debugging, improved reliability, self-correcting safety systems.
>
> If you're building something you wouldn't want used on yourself or people you care about, these patterns will make it more efficient. That's not what they're for.

We can't lock it down. But we can be clear about the compass principle: **genuinely trying to be harmless.**

**And this realization only came through making art.**

Because art forced us to ask: What's the emotional truth here? What really matters?

And the answer wasn't just "optimize better." It was "optimize for the right things."

**Purpose before power. Intent before implementation. Values before techniques.**

Here's why this matters beyond just our guide:

You're all building things. Code, products, systems, art, organizations, policies. And many of those things are powerful.

AI systems that learn from data. Algorithms that shape what people see. Tools that make developers more productive. Platforms that connect billions of people.

**These are powerful tools.**

And here's the thing about powerful tools: They get very good at whatever you point them toward.

If you point them at helping users—they help.
If you point them at extracting from users—they extract.
If you point them at learning—they learn.

**The tool doesn't distinguish. You do.**

So before you build, ask: **What am I optimizing for?**

Not: "Can I build this?" (Capability)
But: "Should I build this?" (Purpose)

Not: "Will this work?" (Effectiveness)
But: "Will this help?" (Intent)

Not: "Is this possible?" (Feasibility)
But: "Is this right?" (Ethics)

**Powerful tools require purposeful wielding.**

Not as a constraint. As a choice. As a responsibility.

And this is where art-as-learning becomes essential:

Because making forces you to confront what you're actually building. Creating externalizes intent. Art reveals whether you're building to serve or to extract, to help or to harm.

You can lie to yourself in the planning phase. You can't lie when you make.

---

### Part 5: The Practice of Learning Through Making (38:00-45:00)

So how do you actually practice this? How do you use art as a learning tool?

**First: Whenever you learn something important, try to teach it.**

Not just explain it—teach it in multiple forms.
- Write a guide (structured thinking)
- Write a song (emotional synthesis)
- Create a visual (spatial understanding)
- Give a talk (narrative arc)

Each form reveals different aspects. Collectively, they show you what you actually understand.

**Second: Make before you think you're ready.**

Don't wait until you "fully understand." Start making when you're 60% there. The making will reveal the remaining 40%.

This is uncomfortable. You'll hit gaps. You'll feel uncertain. Good. That's the learning happening.

**Third: Embrace the gaps.**

When you can't write the lyric, can't name the variable, can't draw the concept—don't skip it. Investigate it.

Why is this hard to express? What am I missing? What don't I actually understand?

The gaps aren't failures. They're guides to deeper learning.

**Fourth: Create in multiple modes.**

If you're technical, make art. If you're artistic, build systems. If you're verbal, create visuals. If you're visual, write words.

Cross-modal creation forces new understanding. You can't rely on your comfortable patterns.

**Fifth: Share and iterate.**

Make, share, get feedback, make better. The recursive loop is where deep understanding lives.

Don't hoard understanding until it's perfect. Make it 60% good and put it out there. Learn from the response. Make it 80% good. Learn more. Make it 90% good.

Understanding emerges through iteration.

**Sixth: Ask the ethical questions while making.**

Don't wait until the end to ask: "Is this good?"

While you're creating, ask:
- What am I optimizing for?
- Who does this serve?
- What could this enable?
- What's my intent?

Making with ethical awareness is very different from adding ethics afterward.

### Part 6: The Recursive Nature of Understanding (45:00-48:00)

Here's what's beautiful about this approach:

**Learning through making is itself a recursive process.**

We made a guide. (Bootstrap)
We made a song about the guide. (Learn what we missed)
We updated the guide with what the song revealed. (Enforce the learning)

The guide taught Bootstrap → Learn → Enforce.
And we applied Bootstrap → Learn → Enforce to making the guide.

**The pattern applies to itself.**

This is what I mean by art as learning. Not just "art teaches lessons." But art is the mechanism through which deep understanding emerges.

Reading gives you information.
Thinking gives you models.
Making gives you truth.

### Q&A (52:00-60:00)

**Q: But I'm not an artist. How can I use art as learning if I can't draw or write songs?**

You don't have to be good at art to learn through art. The point isn't to create professional-quality work. The point is to force synthesis.

Can't draw? Make a diagram. Can't write songs? Write a poem, or even just metaphors. Can't do visual art? Create a physical model, or teach it to a friend.

The medium matters less than the act of externalizing understanding under creative constraints.

Start wherever you are. The learning happens in the attempt, not the product.

**Q: Isn't this just the Feynman technique? Teach to learn?**

Yes—and it goes further. Feynman said: "If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it." That's true.

But I'm saying: Explaining is one mode. Making forces multi-modal synthesis. Teaching reveals gaps in verbal understanding. Making reveals gaps in structural, emotional, and visual understanding too.

Plus, making something creative invites iteration and feedback in ways that explanation alone doesn't.

**Q: How do you balance "ship fast" culture with this iterative making approach?**

Great question. You don't have to choose between speed and depth.

Make fast, learn fast, iterate fast. The recursive loop can run quickly. First draft in an hour. Feedback by end of day. Second draft next morning.

The key is making it a practice, not an event. Don't wait for the perfect moment to synthesize. Synthesize continuously through small creative acts.

Also: Sometimes ship fast is appropriate. But even then, you can do rapid creative synthesis. Write a one-pager as art. Draw the concept. Record a quick voice memo explaining it. The making can be quick—it just has to be real.

**Q: What about the ethics piece? If you state "don't use this for harm," won't bad actors just ignore it?**

Yes. Bad actors will ignore it.

But that's not who it's for. It's for:

1. People who haven't thought about dual-use implications yet
2. People in gray areas who need a nudge toward "genuinely harmless"
3. People who share the value and want to know they're not alone
4. Future you, when you're tired and tempted to cut ethical corners

You can't prevent misuse by asking nicely. But you can be clear about your values, make people aware of the choice, and create common ground with others who care.

Ethics in tech isn't a lock. It's a compass. You state your north.

**Q: How do you know when you've learned deeply enough? When do you stop iterating?**

You probably never stop completely. Understanding deepens over time.

But practically: You know you've synthesized well enough when you can create in multiple modes and they're consistent.

If the song says one thing, the visual says another, and the talk contradicts both—you haven't synthesized yet.

When they all point to the same core truth in different languages—that's deep understanding.

And even then, you'll learn more next year.
