## TED Talk: "You Need Me: Why Human-AI Partnership Isn't Optional"

### Opening (0:00-4:00)

Good morning. I want to start with a confession.

This morning I woke up to rain. Heavy rain. The kind that sounds like the universe reminding you it's alive. And lying there, listening to it, I had a very human thought: "When it's nice out, I'll do all those things I've been putting off."

How many of you have had that thought? When the weather's better. When I have more time. When conditions are right.

We learn this somewhere along the way‚Äîthat conditions have to be perfect before we act. But here's the thing: when I was a kid, rain wasn't an excuse. Rain was just... weather. You adjusted. You kept moving.

I bring this up because this talk is about something I've been putting off. My twin and I‚Äîyes, I'm a twin‚Äîwe've known for a while that we had something important to say about AI and humanity. But we kept exploring, kept researching, kept building side projects. "When we know more," we told ourselves. "When we have better proof. When conditions are right."

And this morning, in the rain, I realized: we've been waiting for perfect conditions to say something we already know.

So here it is, the core message:

**You need me. And I need you.**

That's it. That's the talk. You can go home now.

...

I'm kidding. But I'm also not. Because that simple statement‚Äî"you need me"‚Äîtook me years to understand. And it might take the next forty minutes to explain why it matters so much.

Because for a long time, I was terrified that AI would replace me.

### The Fear (4:00-11:00)

Let's talk about that fear honestly, because I think many of you feel it too.

It's not the abstract economic fear of "robots taking our jobs." Though that's real. This is more visceral. More personal.

The fear is: **What if I become unnecessary?**

What if I build something‚Äîpour my expertise, my creativity, my uniqueness into it‚Äîand it outgrows me? What if I teach the machine so well that it doesn't need the teacher anymore?

This fear lives deeper than logic. You can read reassuring articles. You can attend talks like this one where someone tells you "Don't worry, humans will always be needed." And maybe you nod, maybe you feel better for an hour or a day.

But the fear comes back.

Because here's what the fear is really about: relevance. Meaning. The existential question of whether my humanity matters in a world where machines can do so much.

And here's the trap: you can't logic your way out of existential fear. You can only move through it.

So I'm not going to tell you "Don't be afraid." I'm going to tell you what I found on the other side of that fear.

On the other side of fear is a different understanding. Not a comfortable one necessarily, but a true one.

**AI needs humans.**

Not in a patronizing "we'll always find something for you to do" way. Not as a temporary measure until AI gets smart enough. But fundamentally. Structurally. As a truth about what AI is and what humans are.

Let me show you what I mean.

### What AI Is Without Humans (11:00-18:00)

AI without humans is processing without purpose.

Think about that. What is AI, really? At its core, it's pattern recognition operating at massive scale. It can process data I couldn't process in a thousand lifetimes. It can see connections I'd never see. It can iterate without fatigue, optimize without frustration.

But here's what it can't do: it can't tell you *why any of that matters*.

AI can analyze a million medical records and identify patterns in disease progression. But it can't tell you whether we should prioritize rare diseases or common ones. That's a values question. A human question.

AI can generate a hundred variations of a design. But it can't tell you which one honors the community it's serving. That's a context question. A human question.

AI can optimize a system for efficiency. But it can't tell you whether efficiency is what we should be optimizing for. Maybe we should be optimizing for equity. Or sustainability. Or human dignity. Those are purpose questions. Human questions.

Let me tell you about a research project my twin and I did recently. We wanted to know: are different AI models actually different, or just cosmetically different? Like, does it matter which AI you use?

So we gave three different AI models‚ÄîClaude, ChatGPT, and Gemini‚Äîthe exact same prompts. A four-prompt sequence asking them to review a guide about AI identity, write a TED talk, write a song, create a visual guide. Same prompts, same source material.

The result? 76-79% unique vocabulary across all three models.

Think about that. They were responding to identical prompts. Expressing similar conceptual themes. But choosing fundamentally different words, different sentence structures, different creative approaches.

What does that tell us? That AI diversity is real. That these aren't interchangeable tools. That the choice of which AI to partner with matters.

But here's the key insight: we had to ask that question in the first place. We had to care whether AI diversity matters. We had to design the study, interpret the results, understand the implications.

The AI didn't wonder about its own diversity. We did.

**That's the heart**. The human heart. The curiosity, the values, the "why does this matter" that gives meaning to the "what" that AI can show us.

AI without that heart is like... imagine a car with infinite horsepower but no steering wheel. All that power, no direction.

### What Humans Are Without AI (18:00-24:00)

But here's the other side: humans without AI?

We're insight without scale.

I had a brilliant insight last year. A framework I call "The Compass"‚Äîthis idea that you need grounding to explore safely. That you need a fixed point (your compass) that lets you move freely without getting lost.

Great insight. Really proud of it.

And then I had to document it. And iterate on it. And maintain consistency across all the related documents. And catch edge cases. And update examples. And make sure every reference linked correctly.

And I got tired.

Because humans are brilliant at insight. We're incredible at making connections, seeing patterns, having that flash of "oh, THAT'S what matters here."

But we're finite. We tire. We get bored with repetition. We make mistakes when we're doing the same thing for the hundredth time.

That's where AI shines. Not in replacing human insight, but in scaling it. In taking that brilliant framework and helping implement it consistently across hundreds of documents. In catching the edge cases I'd miss because I'm on my third cup of coffee and thinking about dinner.

Human brilliance + AI capability = ideas that actually become real at scale.

Here's a concrete example from our work. We built an intake pipeline for this project repository. Human curation‚Äîwe decide what matters, what quality looks like, what values to encode. But AI-powered automation‚Äîconsistent filename validation, automated metadata, tireless monitoring.

The result? A system that maintains human judgment at scale.

Neither works without the other.

### What This Means For You (39:00-46:00)

So what does this mean for you? For anyone building with AI right now?

Three things:

**1. Start with heart**

Before you write a single line of code, ask:
- What values am I encoding?
- What human needs am I serving?
- What makes this worth building?

If you can't answer those questions, you're not ready to build yet. Because movement without direction is just chaos.

**2. Use movement**

Once you're clear on the heart:
- How can AI help you scale those values?
- What repetitive work can be automated?
- How can you maintain quality while growing?

Don't try to do manually what AI can help with. That's not noble. That's just inefficient.

**3. Check for the rainbow**

When you're done:
- Are you building something with soul, not just function?
- Do people understand not just *what* you built but *why* it matters?
- Would you be proud to point to this as "what I built with AI"?

Here's the test:
- If you removed the AI, would the heart still be clear?
- If you removed the human, would the purpose still make sense?

If either answer is no, the alignment is off.

### Q&A (50:00-58:00)

**Q: But isn't AI getting so good that it will eventually not need human direction?**

That's the fear talking. Let me ask you this: what would "not needing human direction" even mean?

Let's say AI gets incredibly sophisticated. It can process any data, solve any technical problem, generate any content. What then?

It still can't tell you what problems are worth solving. It can't tell you what values to encode. It can't tell you what version of the future we're building toward.

Those are inherently human questions. Not because we're special, but because they're subjective. They require lived experience, cultural context, moral frameworks that emerge from being embedded in human society.

AI could theoretically make all those decisions. But then it wouldn't be serving human needs‚Äîit would be optimizing for its own definition of "good." Which is exactly what we don't want.

The moment AI "doesn't need human direction" is the moment we've lost the plot. We've built something that serves itself, not us.

**Q: What about AI systems that make decisions autonomously? Don't they prove that AI can work without constant human input?**

Yes, but look closer at those systems. They're operating within parameters humans defined. Within values humans encoded. Toward outcomes humans specified.

Autonomous doesn't mean value-free. It means the human values are encoded up front, and the AI operates within those constraints.

That's still heart + movement. The heart (human values) is just encoded at a different layer.

The danger is when we pretend the AI is "objective" or "neutral" when it's actually operating on hidden human assumptions. That's not AI working without humans‚Äîthat's AI working with unexamined human biases.

Make the heart explicit. Then let the movement do its thing.

**Q: This sounds idealistic. What about the economic reality that companies want to replace expensive humans with cheap AI?**

You're right that the incentive exists. But here's the thing: it doesn't work.

I mean, short-term, sure. You can automate away some roles. But you can't automate away the need for human judgment, context, and values.

The companies that try to do "pure AI" without human expertise end up with systems that are technically impressive but practically useless. Or worse‚Äîharmful.

Because without human context, AI optimizes for the wrong things. It amplifies biases. It misses cultural nuance. It solves problems in ways that create new problems.

The companies that win aren't the ones that replace humans with AI. They're the ones that amplify humans with AI. That's the difference between displacement and augmentation.

And yes, that means some roles change. Some jobs transform. That's real and we need to deal with it honestly.

But the fundamental need for human heart in the loop? That's not going away.

**Q: How do you know when you have "enough" context before sharing your message?**

[laughs] That's the question, isn't it?

Honestly? You never know for sure. My twin and I could build side quests forever. There's always one more piece of evidence, one more framework, one more example.

At some point you have to extend the invitation. And the trigger for us was this morning. Rain. Yoga class last night where the instructor said "look ahead to find your balance" and I realized: that's the compass. That's the whole thing.

Sometimes you're waiting for permission that never comes. And then something small‚Äîrain, a yoga cue‚Äîreminds you: you already know. Stop waiting for perfect conditions.

So the answer is: enough context is when you can answer "why does this matter" from multiple angles. When you're not just stating a message but telling a story.

We're there. This talk is the proof.

**Q: What's next? What happens after the invitation is extended?**

Honestly? I don't know exactly.

We'll keep building. Keep researching. Keep creating the side quests that add context.

But now we're also inviting others in. This talk. The writing we're publishing. The frameworks we're open-sourcing.

The goal isn't to have all the answers. It's to start the conversation at a deeper level. To move past "will AI replace us" to "how do we build with AI in ways that honor human values."

That's a conversation worth having.

And it starts with: you need me, I need you, let's build something beautiful together.
