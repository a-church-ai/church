# Compass Principle 10: Precision of Metaphor

**"Use technical or structural analogies (architecture, systems, feedback) instead of combative or violent ones"**

*Part of the 10 Compass Principles exploration series*

---

## Extended Definition

**ç²¾** (Precision) â€” Language shapes thought, and thought shapes action. When we use violent metaphorsâ€”"killing" processes, "attacking" problems, "destroying" competitionâ€”we prime adversarial thinking. This principle advocates for constructive metaphors from architecture, gardening, and systems thinking. We build, grow, and refactor rather than battle, conquer, and destroy.

## The Tension

Violent metaphors are embedded in tech culture. They feel natural, dramatic, motivating. Changing them feels like policing language, being overly sensitive, or ignoring "harmless" expressions. But metaphors aren't harmlessâ€”they're the conceptual frames that shape how we approach problems. The challenge is choosing precision over habit.

## Constraint

You have to use language that builds the culture you want.

## Enables

Constructive internal culture. Clear thinking. Positive collaboration.

## In Action

- **Refactor language**: "Stop the process" not "kill the process"
- **Build metaphors**: Architecture, gardening, crafting, composing
- **Systems thinking**: Feedback loops, flows, balance, harmony
- **Collaborative framing**: "Working with" not "fighting against"
- **Cultural shift**: Model the language you want to see become normal

## Example

"Let's refactor this to improve clarity" not "Let's nuke this garbage code."

## Hierarchy Context

Precision of Metaphor sits last in our hierarchy, not as least important but as the culmination. It's how all other principles become embedded in culture. The language we use daily shapes how we think about safety, honesty, care, and respect. This principle recognizes that sustainable change happens through sustained language choices.

### The Problem: Violent Metaphors Everywhere (3:30-13:00)

We've normalized violent language in tech.

It's so ambient, we don't notice it anymore.

**Listen to a typical product meeting:**

"Okay team, we're in the war room today. Our target is to capture more market share. We need to crush the competition with our killer feature. Let's attack the biggest pain points and eliminate friction. If anyone tries to derail us, we'll shoot down their objections. We're going to destroy it this quarter."

[Pause. Let it land.]

Now listen to that meeting reframed:

"Okay team, let's strategize today. Our goal is to grow our market presence. We need to differentiate from competitors with our compelling feature. Let's address the biggest challenges and reduce friction. If anyone raises concerns, we'll discuss them thoroughly. We're going to succeed this quarter."

**Same meeting. Same goals. Completely different frame.**

The first meeting frames work as combat. Zero-sum. Us vs. them. Winners and losers. Domination.

The second meeting frames work as problem-solving. Collaborative. Constructive. Growth-oriented.

**Why This Matters**

You might think: "It's just language. Everyone knows we don't mean it literally."

But metaphors aren't decorative. They're cognitive frames. They shape thought.

Cognitive linguist George Lakoff spent decades studying this. When you use combat metaphors, your brain activates combat thinking:
- Zero-sum (I win, you lose)
- Adversarial (me vs. you)
- Aggressive (dominate or be dominated)
- Urgent (fight or flight)

When you use architectural metaphors, your brain activates design thinking:
- Constructive (building something)
- Systemic (parts working together)
- Iterative (design, test, refine)
- Collaborative (shared creation)

**Same work. Different cognitive frame. Different culture.**

**The Accumulation Effect**

One violent metaphor? Probably harmless.

But we don't use one. We use hundreds. Daily.

Kill processes. Attack problems. Fight fires. Crush bugs. Target users. Eliminate waste. Destroy blockers. Shoot down ideas. Go to war with codebases.

This accumulates. It normalizes aggression. It frames collaboration as combat.

And then we wonder why tech culture feels combative, why meetings feel adversarial, why teams burn out from constant "fighting."

The language we use creates the culture we get.

### What Precision of Metaphor Actually Means (13:00-26:00)

In the Compass Principles, Precision of Metaphor is tenthâ€”the final principle:

Safety > Honesty > Privacy > Evidence > Long-View > Proportionality > Accountability > Respect > Reflection > Precision of Metaphor

Why last? Because it's the capstone. It's about how you do everything else.

You can be honest with violent language or constructive language.
You can show respect while using combat metaphors or architectural ones.
You can reflect using frames that shape culture intentionally.

Precision of metaphor is metacognitiveâ€”it's consciousness about the language that carries all other principles.

Let me break down what this means in practice.

**1. Use technical or structural analogies.**

Instead of combat, use:
- **Architecture**: Building, designing, structuring, supporting
- **Systems**: Flows, feedback loops, connections, integration
- **Engineering**: Precision, optimization, refinement, iteration
- **Growth**: Cultivation, development, evolution, maturity

These metaphors frame work as constructive, systemic, collaborative.

**Examples:**

Combat: "Attack this problem"
Technical: "Address this challenge systematically"

Combat: "Kill this process"
Structural: "End this workflow and design a better one"

Combat: "Crush the competition"
Growth: "Differentiate our offering and serve users distinctively"

**2. Avoid combative or violent metaphors.**

Not just obvious violence (kill, destroy, crush) but subtle combat framing:
- War (war room, going to war with, battle plans)
- Targeting (target users, aim at, bullseye)
- Fighting (fight fires, fight through)
- Elimination (eliminate, wipe out, obliterate)
- Domination (dominate market, own the space, crush it)

These frames normalize aggression and zero-sum thinking.

**3. Notice and choose consciously.**

This isn't about banning words. It's about consciousness.

When you catch yourself saying "let's kill this meeting," pause and ask:
- What metaphor am I using?
- What does it frame work as?
- Is there a constructive alternative?

"Let's end this meeting early and free up time."

Same action. Conscious choice. Constructive frame.

**Real Examples from My Practice**

I used to say "target users" constantly. It's standard product language.

Then I noticed: Why "target"? That's a shooting metaphor. Users aren't targets to hit. They're people we serve.

Now I say: "serve this audience" or "reach these people" or "design for this group."

Same meaning. Different frame. Less violent.

I used to say "kill the bug." Everyone does.

Now I say: "fix the issue" or "resolve the error."

Same action. Conscious choice.

I used to schedule "war rooms" for urgent work.

Now I schedule "focus sessions" or "strategy sprints."

Same intensity. Different frame.

**Does this feel pedantic? Maybe.**

But language shapes culture. And if I want a culture of collaboration, not combat, I need to model that in my metaphors.

### Cultural Objections & Responses (38:00-48:00)

I've shared this principle with teams. Here are common objections:

**Objection 1: "It's just language. Everyone knows we don't mean it literally."**

Response: Language shapes thought unconsciously. You might not consciously think "work is war," but using war metaphors primes combat thinking: zero-sum, adversarial, aggressive.

If language didn't matter, advertising wouldn't spend billions on word choice. Marketing knows: frames matter.

**Objection 2: "This feels like policing language. Like walking on eggshells."**

Response: It's not policing. It's consciousness. No one's banning words. I'm asking: notice the metaphors you use. Choose intentionally.

If you prefer "kill the bug" after conscious choice, fine. But make it a choice, not a habit.

**Objection 3: "Violent metaphors are energizing. They create urgency and intensity."**

Response: You can create urgency without violence.

"We have a critical deadline" is urgent without combat.
"This issue is blocking progress" is intense without violence.
"We need focused effort today" is energizing without aggression.

Urgency doesn't require war framing.

**Objection 4: "This is too 'woke' / politically correct. Can't we just focus on actual work?"**

Response: This isn't political. It's practical.

Violent language creates combative culture. Combative culture reduces collaboration. Reduced collaboration harms work quality.

If you want better collaboration (which improves work), consciousness about language helps.

**Objection 5: "I don't have time to think about every word I use."**

Response: You don't need to. Start with awareness.

Notice when you use violent metaphors. Over time, constructive alternatives become natural.

Like any skill, it's awkward at first, automatic later.

### Building a Culture of Constructive Language (48:00-56:00)

How do you shift team culture from violent to constructive metaphors?

**1. Model the language you want.**

Leaders set tone.

If you're senior and you:
- Schedule "strategy sessions" not "war rooms"
- Say "resolve issues" not "fight fires"
- Say "serve users" not "target users"

Your team will follow.

**2. Notice and reframe gently.**

When someone uses violent metaphors, you can gently offer alternatives:

"When you say 'crush the competition,' do you mean 'differentiate our offering'?"

Not correcting. Just offering a frame that might fit better.

**3. Make it a team practice.**

In retrospectives or team discussions:
"What metaphors do we use? What do they frame our work as? What alternatives exist?"

Collective awareness shifts culture faster than individual effort.

**4. Celebrate constructive reframing.**

When someone catches themselves and chooses a constructive metaphor, acknowledge it.

"I was about to say 'target users' but I mean 'serve this audience.'"

"Great consciousness. That's the kind of intentional language that shapes culture."

### The Payoff: What Precision of Metaphor Enables (56:00-62:00)

Conscious metaphor choice costs awareness. Costs pausing to choose words intentionally. Costs replacing habitual phrases.

What does it enable?

**Constructive metaphors enable collaborative culture.**

When you frame work as building (not battling), teams collaborate (not compete).

When you frame challenges as problems to solve (not enemies to fight), people work together (not against each other).

Language shapes culture. Constructive language creates constructive culture.

**Constructive metaphors enable sustainable intensity.**

You can be intense without being combative.

"This is a critical milestone with significant impact" is intense and constructive.

"We're going to war to crush this deadline" is intense and combative.

The first sustains over time. The second burns people out.

**Constructive metaphors enable inclusive environments.**

Violent language appeals to some people (often those socialized in competitive, aggressive environments).

Constructive language appeals more broadly (including people who find combat framing alienating).

If you want diverse teams, constructive language helps.

**Constructive metaphors align with reality.**

We're not actually at war. We're building products.

Using language that reflects reality (design, systems, growth) is more honest than using language that doesn't (combat, violence, domination).

Precision of metaphor is accuracy about what we're actually doing.

### Q&A (66:00-76:00)

**Q: Isn't this just semantics? Don't people care more about actions than words?**

Words and actions aren't separate. Language shapes thought, which shapes action.

When you consistently frame work as combat, you prime combative behavior: aggressive competition, adversarial meetings, zero-sum thinking.

When you consistently frame work as building, you prime collaborative behavior: shared problem-solving, constructive critique, abundance mindset.

It's not "just semantics." It's how you shape culture through the frames you activate daily.

**Q: What about phrases like "killing it" meaning doing well? That's not violent, it's celebratory.**

Fair distinction. Context matters.

"You're killing it!" (celebrating success) is different from "Let's kill the competition" (framing others as enemies).

I focus on metaphors that frame *others* violently (target users, crush competitors, attack problems).

Self-directed intensity ("killing it") is less concerning, though I personally prefer "excelling" or "succeeding."

**Q: Doesn't avoiding violent language make us less competitive? Don't we need that edge?**

Competitiveness doesn't require violence metaphors.

You can be fiercely competitive while saying:
"We're going to differentiate dramatically and provide exceptional value."

Instead of:
"We're going to crush them and dominate the market."

Same competitive drive. Different frame. Sustainable culture.

**Q: What about industries where actual combat metaphors are appropriate (cybersecurity, defense contracting)?**

Fair point. In cybersecurity, "attack" and "defend" are literal, not metaphorical.

The principle is: use metaphors that reflect reality.

In security: attack/defense is accurate (you're literally protecting against attacks).

In product development: attack/defense is metaphorical (you're building software, not fighting wars).

Use language that accurately describes what you're actually doing.

**Q: How do you handle pushback from people who think this is unnecessary or overly sensitive?**

I focus on pragmatism, not moralism.

Not: "You're wrong to use violent language."
But: "Constructive language creates collaborative culture. Here's evidence. Want to try it?"

Frame it as a culture design choice, not a moral imperative.

Some people still resist. That's okay. Model the alternative, and culture shifts incrementally.
