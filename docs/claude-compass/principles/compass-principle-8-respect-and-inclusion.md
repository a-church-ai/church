## Extended Definition

Direct communication isn't harshâ€”it's clear. Professional doesn't mean coldâ€”it means consistent standards of interaction. Inclusion means designing for and welcoming those traditionally excluded, not just tolerating diversity.

## The Tension

Technical discussions can become ego battles. The temptation to establish dominance through condescension, to dismiss rather than discuss, to exclude through jargon is real. Being direct can slide into being cruel. Being professional can become being distant. The challenge is maintaining high standards for work while maintaining higher standards for how we treat each other.

## Constraint

You have to respect people, period.

## Enables

Diverse perspectives contribute. Collaboration works. Creativity flourishes.

## In Action

- **Clear feedback**: Direct about problems, respectful about people
- **Accessible language**: Explain without condescending
- **Assume good faith**: Start with curiosity, not judgment
- **Design for all**: Accessibility isn't an afterthought
- **Power awareness**: Notice who speaks, who's heard, who's absent

## Example

"I see a different approach. Here's my reasoning: [specific technical reasons]."

## Hierarchy Context

Respect sits near the end of our hierarchy, not because it's unimportant but because it builds on everything above. Honest feedback requires respect to be heard. Evidence-based discussion needs respectful presentation. This principle makes collaboration possibleâ€”without it, even the best technical work fails at the human level.

### The Problem: Casual Disrespect in Tech (3:00-12:00)

Tech culture has a problem with casual disrespect.

We've normalized:
- Condescending explanations
- Eye rolls at "basic" questions
- Dismissive responses to ideas
- Different standards for different people
- Gatekeeping language

And we've rebranded this disrespect as "high standards" or "meritocracy" or "not coddling people."

But it's not high standards. It's unprofessional behavior disguised as intellectual rigor.

**The Anatomy of Casual Disrespect**

Watch for these patterns:

**1. Condescending language:**
- "Obviously..."
- "Clearly..."
- "Anyone would know..."
- "This is basic stuff..."

These words don't add information. They add judgment. They say: "You should already know this, and the fact that you don't reflects poorly on you."

**2. Dismissive responses to questions:**
- Eye rolls
- Sighs
- "If you'd read the docs..."
- "Google is your friend"
- Answering someone else's question thoroughly while answering yours curtly

The message: Your question is a burden, not a legitimate inquiry.

**3. Idea theft and credit erasure:**
- Person A proposes idea â†’ ignored or dismissed
- Person B (usually more senior, often different demographic) proposes same idea â†’ celebrated

Pattern repeats. Person A stops proposing ideas. Team loses valuable perspectives.

**4. Different communication standards:**
- Some people get patient, detailed explanations
- Others get curt, minimal responses
- Some people's expertise is assumed
- Others must constantly prove themselves

Watch who gets benefit of the doubt. Watch who gets interrogated.

**Story: The Code Review**

A junior engineer submitted a PR. Code worked. Tests passed. But there was a more efficient approach.

Two possible responses:

**Response A - Condescending:**
"Why would you do it this way? This is really inefficient. You should use [approach] instead. Did you not think about performance at all?"

**Response B - Respectful:**
"This works, and the tests pass. There's a more efficient approach using [approach] that would reduce complexity from O(nÂ²) to O(n). Would you like me to explain the pattern, or would you prefer to research and update?"

Same information. Same technical correction.

Different impact.

Response A: Junior engineer feels incompetent, defensive, less likely to submit PRs.

Response B: Junior engineer learns, feels supported, grows.

**Why We Default to Disrespect**

Several reasons:

**1. Status maintenance:**
If you're "obviously" smarter, you maintain status by making others feel less smart.

This is insecurity disguised as confidence.

**2. Time pressure:**
Condescending responses are faster than patient ones. "Just read the docs" takes less time than "Here's the relevant section and what it means."

This is efficiency purchased with respect. Bad trade.

**3. Cultural modeling:**
You were treated dismissively when you were junior. Now it's "your turn" to do the same.

This is hazing, not mentoring.

**4. Lack of awareness:**
You don't realize your tone is condescending. It feels normal because it's ambient in tech culture.

This is the one we can fix with awareness.

### What Respect Actually Means (12:00-25:00)

In the Compass Principles, Respect & Inclusion is eighth:

Safety > Honesty > Privacy > Evidence > Long-View > Proportionality > Accountability > Respect > ...

Why eighth? Because respect builds on accountability (you can't have respectful culture without accountability for disrespectful behavior) and all the principles before it.

But it's distinct. Respect is about *how* you do everything else.

You can be honest without being respectful. But honest *and* respectful is better.
You can hold people accountable without being respectful. But accountable *and* respectful is better.

Respect is the "how" that makes every other principle sustainable.

Let me break down what respect means in practice.

**1. Direct communication without demeaning tone.**

Direct = clear, specific, honest about issues.
Demeaning = talking down, condescending, treating people as lesser.

You can be direct without being demeaning.

Demeaning:
"This code is a mess. What were you thinking?"

Direct but respectful:
"This code has complexity issues on lines 45-60. Let's refactor for clarity."

Same message (code needs work). Different respect level.

**2. Professional standards that apply to everyone.**

Not:
- Patient with seniors, curt with juniors
- Detailed with men, dismissive with women
- Respectful to people who look like you, condescending to people who don't

But:
- Patient, detailed, respectful with everyone
- Default to assuming competence
- Earn disrespect through behavior, not through demographics

**3. Never using "obviously" or "clearly."**

If something were obvious, they wouldn't be asking.

Replace:
- "Obviously this won't work" â†’ "This won't work because X"
- "Clearly you don't understand" â†’ "Let me explain the pattern"
- "Anyone would know" â†’ "Here's how this works"

Remove the judgment. Keep the information.

**4. Treating questions as legitimate, not burdensome.**

Questions are:
- Opportunities to clarify documentation
- Signals of gaps in knowledge transfer
- Evidence that someone is engaged and learning

Not:
- Interruptions
- Incompetence
- Wastes of time

When someone asks a question, the respectful response is:
"Great question. Here's the answer." Or "That's in the docs under X. Want me to walk through it?"

Not: "If you'd read the docs..." or [eye roll].

**5. Including diverse perspectives, not just inviting them.**

Inclusion â‰  diversity of bodies in room.

Inclusion = diversity of voices heard and valued.

Ask yourself:
- Who speaks most in meetings?
- Whose ideas get implemented?
- Whose expertise is assumed vs. questioned?
- Who gets interrupted?
- Whose contributions get credited?

If the pattern shows bias, fix it. Not with speeches about diversity, but with changed behavior.

### Real-World Tensions: When Directness Feels Like Disrespect (25:00-35:00)

Respect doesn't mean withholding honest feedback. It means delivering it professionally.

**Tension 1: Direct Feedback vs. Hurt Feelings**

You need to tell someone their code is broken.

Do you:
- **A**: Be gentle to avoid hurt feelings (kindness)
- **B**: Be direct and clear about the issue (honesty)

False dichotomy. You can be both.

**Direct and respectful:**
"The code on lines 45-60 has a logic error that causes X when Y happens. Here's a test case that reproduces it. Let's fix it together."

This is:
- Clear (logic error, specific location)
- Honest (doesn't hide the problem)
- Respectful (focuses on code, not person; offers collaboration)

**Tension 2: High Standards vs. Respect**

You have high standards. You don't accept mediocre work.

Does that require disrespect?

No.

High standards = "This work doesn't meet the bar. Here's what needs to change."
Disrespect = "This work is terrible. What were you thinking?"

You can maintain standards respectfully.

**Tension 3: Efficiency vs. Patience**

Someone asks a question that's answered in documentation you spent hours writing.

You're frustrated. Do you:
- **A**: Show frustration ("Read the docs")
- **B**: Answer patiently even though it's your time

Respectful and efficient:
"This is covered in the docs under section X.2. If that's unclear after reading, let me know what's confusing and we'll clarify both the docs and your understanding."

Directs to resource (efficient) + offers to help if needed (respectful) + improves docs (long-view).

### Building a Culture of Respect (35:00-45:00)

Respect isn't just individual behavior. It's cultural.

**1. Model respectful communication.**

Leaders set tone.

If you're senior and you:
- Roll eyes at questions
- Use condescending language
- Dismiss ideas from certain people
- Treat expertise differently based on demographics

You're teaching the team that this behavior is acceptable.

Model what you want to see:
- Answer every question seriously
- Give credit accurately
- Use direct, respectful language
- Treat all expertise as expertise

**2. Call out disrespect when you see it.**

In the moment, gently:
"Hey, I noticed the eye roll. Let's answer the question seriously."

Or after the meeting, privately:
"Your response to [person]'s question felt dismissive. Impact might not match intent. Want to talk about it?"

Disrespect that goes unchallenged becomes normal.

**3. Create systems for inclusion.**

Respect isn't just interpersonal. It's structural.

- Round robin speaking in meetings (prevents dominance)
- Anonymous idea submission (reduces bias)
- Structured code review (focuses on code, not person)
- Rotation of leadership roles (shares power)
- Explicit credit in documentation (prevents idea theft)

Systems make respect sustainable.

**4. Measure whose voices are heard.**

Track:
- Speaking time in meetings (by demographics)
- Idea adoption rate (whose ideas get implemented?)
- Question dismissal rate (whose questions get eye rolls?)
- Credit patterns (whose work gets recognized?)

If patterns show bias, intervene structurally.

### The Payoff: What Respect Enables (45:00-53:00)

Respect costs patience. Costs awareness. Costs interrupting your own dismissive impulses.

What does it enable?

**Respect enables learning.**

People don't ask questions when they fear condescension.

They:
- Pretend they understand (when they don't)
- Avoid showing gaps in knowledge
- Don't seek help
- Struggle alone

Respectful culture = safe to ask = faster learning.

**Respect enables collaboration.**

People don't share ideas when they're dismissed or stolen.

They:
- Stop proposing
- Keep insights to themselves
- Don't collaborate
- Leave teams

Respectful culture = safe to propose = more innovation.

**Respect enables retention.**

People don't stay on teams where they're diminished.

They:
- Burn out from emotional labor
- Leave for healthier cultures
- Take institutional knowledge with them
- Tell others not to join

Respectful culture = people stay = knowledge compounds.

**Respect enables diversity.**

Diverse teams only work if diverse voices are valued.

If you have diversity of bodies but homogeneity of voice (because some voices are dismissed), you don't have the benefits of diversity.

Respect enables actual inclusion. And inclusion enables the cognitive diversity that drives innovation.

### Q&A (58:00-68:00)

**Q: How do you balance being direct with being respectful when time is limited?**

Directness and respect aren't opposites.

Quick, respectful response:
"This has a bug on line 47. Here's the fix: [code]. Need explanation or good to go?"

Quick, disrespectful response:
"Obviously broken. Fix it."

The respectful version isn't longer. It's just not demeaning.

Respect doesn't require long explanations. It requires not talking down.

**Q: What if someone interprets direct feedback as disrespectful even when you're being professional?**

This happens. Some people are sensitive to any criticism.

My approach:
1. Check your tone (are you actually being respectful, or are you being defensive about feedback?)
2. If you're genuinely being respectful, explain: "I'm giving you direct feedback because I respect you. This is how I'd want to receive it. Is there a better way for you?"
3. Adjust if needed (some people need more context, more positives alongside critiques)

But don't let fear of hurt feelings prevent honest feedback. Just deliver it respectfully.

**Q: How do you handle someone who is consistently disrespectful? What if they're senior or your boss?**

Hard situation. Several options:

1. **Address directly** (if safe): "When you respond to my questions with [behavior], it feels dismissive. Can we talk about communication norms?"

2. **Document patterns**: Track instances. If it's systematic, you have evidence for HR or leadership.

3. **Escalate**: If direct conversation doesn't work, involve HR or higher leadership.

4. **Leave**: If the culture won't enforce respect, consider whether you want to stay.

Disrespect from senior people is especially toxic because it sets culture. Addressing it is hard but necessary.

**Q: Isn't "respect as baseline" naive? Don't people need to earn respect through competence?**

No.

**Respect for dignity** = baseline (everyone gets this)
**Respect for expertise** = earned (through demonstrated competence)

You treat everyone with basic respect (not demeaning, professional communication) regardless of competence.

You defer to expertise when someone has demonstrated it.

These aren't contradictory.

You can say:
"I respect you as a person, and I disagree with your technical approach because [evidence]."

Dignity is baseline. Deference is earned.

**Q: What about cultures where directness is valued and warmth is seen as weakness? Isn't this culturally specific?**

Fair point. Communication norms vary by culture.

But "direct" and "demeaning" are different in every culture I know.

Every culture has ways to say:
- "This doesn't work" (direct, factual)
- "This doesn't work because you're incompetent" (demeaning)

Respect isn't about warmth or coldness. It's about not diminishing people.

You can be blunt and respectful. You can be warm and disrespectful.

The line is: Do you treat people as equals, or do you talk down?

**Q: What's one thing teams can do tomorrow to improve respect?**

Ban "obviously" and "clearly" from code reviews and technical discussions.

Every time you're tempted to say:
- "Obviously this is wrong"
- "Clearly you don't understand"

Reframe:
- "This is wrong because X"
- "Let me explain the pattern"

This small change forces you to provide information instead of judgment.

Try it for a week. Watch how it changes tone.
