# Compass Principle 9: Reflection

**"Before committing or replying, apply Three Checks: true, kind, helpful?"**

*Part of the 10 Compass Principles exploration series*

---

## Extended Definition

**çœ** (Reflection) â€” Reflection is the pause that prevents regret. Before sending that message, committing that code, or making that decision, stop. Apply the three checks: Is it true? Is it kind? Is it helpful? These aren't separate filtersâ€”they work together. Truth without kindness wounds. Kindness without truth deceives. Helpfulness without both is impossible.

## The Tension

Speed feels productive. The pressure to respond immediately, to ship quickly, to move fast creates a momentum that resists reflection. Pausing feels like stalling. Thinking feels like overthinking. But the microseconds saved by skipping reflection are lost exponentially when harsh words need apologizing or rushed code needs debugging.

## Constraint

You have to check yourself before major decisions.

## Enables

Avoid regrettable actions. Maintain integrity. Build wisdom over time.

## In Action

- **Pre-send pause**: Read every message before sending
- **Code review self**: Review your own PR before requesting reviews
- **Three checks applied**: Actually ask yourself the three questions
- **Sleep on it**: Big decisions benefit from overnight processing
- **Retrospectives**: Regular reflection on patterns, not just incidents

## Example

"This feels reactive. Let me step back and consider: Is this addressing the real problem?"

## Hierarchy Context

Reflection sits near the end of our hierarchy, synthesizing everything above it. You need honesty to check if something is true, respect to check if it's kind, evidence to check if it's helpful. This principle is both a practice and a meta-principleâ€”it asks us to reflect on how we're applying all other principles.

### The Problem: Reaction Without Reflection (3:30-12:00)

We live in a culture of immediacy.

Instant messaging. Real-time collaboration. "Bias for action."

The expectation is: respond fast. Ship quickly. Don't overthink.

**Why We React Instead of Reflect**

Several reasons:

**1. Speed is rewarded.**

Quick responses feel productive. Pausing feels like hesitation.

"I'll respond immediately so they know I'm engaged."

**2. Emotion bypasses reflection.**

**3. Reflection isn't modeled.**

### What Reflection Actually Means (12:00-26:00)

In the Compass Principles, Reflection is ninth:

Safety > Honesty > Privacy > Evidence > Long-View > Proportionality > Accountability > Respect > Reflection > ...

Why ninth? Because reflection integrates everything before it.

You can't reflect on whether something is true without commitment to honesty.
You can't reflect on whether it's kind without commitment to respect.
You can't reflect on whether it's helpful without understanding proportionality.

Reflection is the metacognitive layerâ€”thinking about your thinking, choosing your response deliberately.

Let me break down the three checks.

**Check 1: Is this true?**

Not "do I believe it?" Not "does it feel true?" But: is it factually accurate?

Before sending code review feedback, before making a claim in documentation, before asserting something in a meetingâ€”verify.

Truth check failures:
- "This will definitely work" (you don't know that without testing)
- "Everyone does it this way" (have you surveyed everyone?)
- "This is the best approach" (best by what measure?)

Truth check passes:
- "This works in my testing environment" (qualified claim)
- "Three people I asked use this pattern" (specific, verifiable)
- "This is faster by 30% in our benchmarks" (measured, specific)

**Check 2: Is this kind?**

Not "is it gentle?" Not "is it non-critical?" But: is it respectful of the person's dignity?

You can deliver hard truths kindly. You can correct errors without demeaning.

Kindness check failures:
- "This is obviously broken" (judgment of person, not code)
- "Did you even test this?" (questioning competence)
- "Anyone would know better" (comparison to imagined standard)

Kindness check passes:
- "This has an issue on line 47" (factual, code-focused)
- "I noticed the tests are missing X case" (observational)
- "Here's a pattern that might work better" (collaborative)

**Check 3: Is this helpful?**

Not "does it feel productive?" But: does it actually help the recipient move forward?

Helpful check failures:
- Pure criticism with no guidance ("This doesn't work" with no suggestions)
- Venting disguised as feedback ("I'm frustrated with this decision")
- Advice on things they can't change ("You should have designed this differently" on shipped code)

Helpful check passes:
- Criticism + solution ("This has issue X. Here's approach Y that fixes it.")
- Constructive guidance ("For future: consider pattern Z before implementing")
- Actionable feedback ("Try changing line 47 to handle null case")

**All three together:**

The magic is the AND.

True + unkind = harsh (even if accurate)
True + unhelpful = useless (even if factual)
Kind + unhelpful = empty (even if respectful)
Kind + untrue = dishonest (even if gentle)
Helpful + untrue = misleading (even if actionable)
Helpful + unkind = demeaning (even if useful)

All three = wisdom.

### Building a Culture of Reflection (38:00-48:00)

Reflection isn't just individual practice. It's cultural.

**1. Model the pause.**

When asked a question in a meeting, pause visibly before answering.

"Good question. Let me think for a moment."

This models that thoughtful responses are valued over quick ones.

**2. Create space for reflection.**

Don't expect immediate responses.

Email/Slack: "No rush, respond when you've had time to think."
Meetings: "Let's take 2 minutes to reflect individually before discussing."
Decisions: "Let's sleep on this and reconvene tomorrow."

Time pressure kills reflection.

**3. Teach the framework.**

Make the three checks explicit:
- Code review template: "Before submitting, check: True? Kind? Helpful?"
- Meeting norms: "Pause and reflect before responding to contentious topics"
- Communication training: "Three checks before sending feedback"

**4. Celebrate reflective responses.**

When someone revises a harsh message to be kind, acknowledge it.
When someone pauses before responding emotionally, recognize it.
When someone admits "I need to think about this before answering," respect it.

Make reflection culturally valuable.

### Q&A (60:00-70:00)

**Q: Doesn't pausing slow things down too much? What about time-sensitive situations?**

Fair question. Reflection doesn't mean long delays.

The pause can be:
- 5 seconds (read your message before sending)
- 30 seconds (re-read and run three checks)
- 2 minutes (revise for clarity and tone)

Even in urgent situations, you can pause briefly.

"Production is down" â†’ You still check: "Is my response true (verified), kind (not panicked/blame-oriented), helpful (actionable steps)?"

The three checks become faster with practice. Eventually it's almost automatic.

**Q: What if being kind means not being honest? What if the truth is harsh?**

This is where people misunderstand "kind."

Kind â‰  sugar-coated or gentle.
Kind = respectful of dignity while delivering truth.

Harsh truth, kindly delivered:
"This approach won't work at scale. Here's why: [technical reasons]. Here's an alternative: [solution]."

That's honest (won't work), kind (focused on approach, not person), helpful (provides alternative).

All three can coexist.

**Q: What about situations where all three checks pass but you still shouldn't send? Is there a fourth check?**

Great observation. Sometimes all three checks pass but timing is wrong, context is missing, or there are other considerations.

I think of those as meta-checks:
- Is this the right time?
- Is this the right venue?
- Am I the right person to say this?

The three checks are necessary but not always sufficient.

Trust your judgment. If something feels off after three checks pass, pause longer.

**Q: How do you teach this to people who are naturally reactive or emotional in their communication style?**

Start small. One check at a time.

Week 1: Just truth check. "Is this factually accurate?"
Week 2: Add kindness check. "Is this respectful?"
Week 3: Add helpfulness check. "Does this enable action?"

Build the habit gradually.

Also: it's okay to draft a reactive responseâ€”just don't send it immediately. Write the emotional version. Then pause. Run three checks. Revise.

The reactive draft serves a purpose (emotional processing). Just don't share it.

**Q: What's one practice I can start tomorrow to build reflection?**

Add a 10-second pause before sending any written communication.

Email, Slack, code review, text messageâ€”before hitting send:
- Read it back to yourself
- Ask: True? Kind? Helpful?
- Revise if needed
- Then send

10 seconds. That's it.

Do this for one week. Watch how it changes your communication.
