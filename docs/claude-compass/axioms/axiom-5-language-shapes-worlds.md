## Core Concept

## Why It Matters

In human-AI collaboration, language is our primary interface. The metaphors we chooseâ€”warfare vs. gardening, targeting vs. servingâ€”shape both development culture and user relationships. AI systems trained on violent metaphors may generate aggressive responses. Humans using combative language may build adversarial systems. Our words architect the worlds we inhabit.

## In Practice

- **Choose constructive metaphors**: "Refactor" not "kill," "serve users" not "target users"
- **Audit internal language**: What metaphors dominate your documentation and discussions?
- **Design linguistic interfaces**: Error messages, prompts, and responses shape user experience
- **Notice framing effects**: How does "bug" vs. "issue" vs. "opportunity" change approach?
- **Language as product**: Treat every word in your system as intentional design

### Part 1: Metaphors Shape Thinking (5:00-22:00)

**The Linguistic Relativity Hypothesis** (Sapir-Whorf)

The language you speak influences how you think.

This isn't just philosophyâ€”there's research backing this.

- Russian speakers distinguish light blue and dark blue as different colors (not shades)â€”and they perceive them as more distinct than English speakers do
- Languages without relative directions (left/right) but only cardinal directions (north/south) produce speakers with better spatial orientation
- The metaphors you use shape your conceptual frameworks

**Applied to Technology:**

If you talk about users as targets, you start thinking about them as targets.

If you talk about features as things to kill, you start thinking about work as violent.

If you talk about markets as battlefields, you start thinking about business as war.

And those thought patternsâ€”they shape your design decisions.

**Example 1: "Target Users"**

Common phrase. Seems innocent.

But "target" is a combat metaphor. You target enemies. You target threats.

When you say "target users," you're subtly positioning users as:
- Objects (not subjects)
- Things to be acted upon (not collaborators)
- Marks to hit (not people to serve)

Now watch what happens in design:

**Targeting mindset:**
- How do we capture them?
- How do we keep them on the page?
- How do we convert them?
- How do we maximize engagement (even if it's not healthy)?

**Serving mindset:**
- How do we help them?
- How do we provide value?
- How do we earn their trust?
- How do we respect their time and agency?

Same product. Different approach. Because the metaphor shaped the thinking.

**Example 2: "Kill the Feature"**

Super common in tech. "Let's kill that feature. It's not performing."

But "kill" is a violent metaphor.

And when you use violent language casually, it normalizes aggression.

Watch:

**Violent language culture:**
- "Kill the feature"

- "Blow away the old data"
- "Execute the process"
- Aggressive, destructive, careless

**Constructive language culture:**
- "End the feature"
- "Clear the cache"

The second version isn't just "nicer." It's more precise. And precision in language leads to precision in thought.

**Example 3: "Dominate the Market"**

**Value-creation mindset:**
- How do we serve users better than anyone else?
- How do we differentiate on value?
- How do we contribute to the ecosystem?
- Positive-sum thinking: rising tide lifts all boats

Same business goals (revenue, growth). Different strategies. Because the metaphor shaped the approach.

### Part 3: Metaphor as Design Surface (42:00-62:00)

Okay, so language matters. Now what?

**Treat your metaphors like you treat your UI.**

**Step 1: Inventory Your Language**

**Step 2: Classify Metaphors**

**Violent/Combat metaphors:**
- Kill, attack, destroy, nuke, force, dominate, crush, eliminate, terminate, target, capture, etc.

**Constructive metaphors:**
- Build, serve, create, invite, address, provide, collaborate, support, end, clear, etc.

**Neutral/Technical metaphors:**
- Run, execute, process, calculate, etc.

**Step 3: Replace Violent with Constructive**

**Violent â†’ Constructive Replacements:**

**Q: "Isn't this just political correctness? Doesn't it stifle creativity and directness?"**

"Kill the feature" isn't more direct than "End the feature." They're the same length, same meaning.

**Q: "What about technical terms that are established? Like 'kill a process' is standard Unix terminology."**

Fair point. Some terms are so embedded they're hard to change.

In a codebase or terminal: `kill -9` is fineâ€”it's technical, established, precise.

In a meeting or product description: "Let's kill this feature" â†’ choose something more precise.

- Change your own language first
